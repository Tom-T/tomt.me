Case study · COBOL → Go on Kubernetes

Strangling a card platform off the mainframe

Part of the problem with legacy modernization is that everyone starts with the tech. New language, new cloud, big rewrite... and two years later the old system is still running, because nobody could prove the new one did the same thing. So I started somewhere else - with the business case, and with a way to prove parity before cutting anything over.

View the project on GitHub Schedule a call

What this is, honestly. This is a representative simulation, not a real employer's system. The legacy code is adapted from AWS's open-source COBOL CardDemo, run on GnuCOBOL/Linux because I can't (and shouldn't) get live financial-institution data or a real mainframe. The company, the cast, and the dollar figures are illustrative. The engineering marked "built" below is real and runs. Everything else is labeled as scaffolded or simulated, on purpose.

The business case comes first

The tech choice is downstream of the business problem, so that's where I start. The scenario: a reliable but aging COBOL card platform, maintained by a shrinking, retiring team. Four people hold the core knowledge. Three of them retire inside about 30 months. One of them, after 31 years, owns the interest, settlement, and posting logic that "nobody else fully understands."

That's the whole reason the project has urgency - not a language preference, a retirement cliff. For a PCI-scoped payment system, a batch defect is a compliance and financial-loss event, not an inconvenience. So the first slice deliberately targets the retiring engineer's program: get the highest-risk, least-understood logic out from under a single person, first, while he's still here to check the work.

The approach: strangler fig

No big-bang rewrite. Stand a facade in front of the legacy batch, replace one capability at a time with a Go service, and cut over only after a golden-master parity test proves the new output matches the old, field for field. Every slice is reversible. If a slice can't prove parity, it doesn't ship - the test is the gate, not my confidence.

There are two seams, and I treat them differently on purpose. The batch side is a true strangle - containerize the COBOL, then replace it program by program behind the router. The online side (the old terminal screens) is an honest rebuild in Go + HTMX, not a line-by-line port, because the Linux COBOL runtime has no equivalent for the mainframe's screen layer. Saying that out loud, instead of pretending it's a clean port, is part of the point.

One slice, proven at parity

Rather than a wide, shallow demo, the target I set was one thin vertical slice, taken all the way through: the interest batch. My hands-on part is the enablement - I stood up the platform the slice runs on, and I defined the parity contract the work has to satisfy. On that foundation, the AI dev team built the Go engine. It does money math in fixed-point decimal, never floating point, and it mirrors the original down to the small quirks - the preserved last-account edge case, and truncation instead of rounding, because the COBOL doesn't round and fidelity to the legacy behavior beats "cleaner" math when a parity test is the contract.

A golden-master harness diffs every field of the Go output against the frozen COBOL output. It's green, and it runs in CI on every change that touches the batch or the golden master, so the parity gate can't silently rot. That's the thesis in miniature: I don't get to claim the rewrite is correct - the parity test does, on every commit.

Built, deficient, or next - drawn on purpose

This is the part most portfolios skip. A portfolio piece is more credible when it draws the line clearly than when it implies everything is production-hot. So here's exactly where that line is today.

Built and tested runs today

  • A local platform that stands up with one command - Terraform provisions a small libvirt/k3s Kubernetes lab, and Kustomize applies Postgres, the object store, and the interest batch as a scheduled job.
  • The COBOL baseline compiles and runs the interest batch on GnuCOBOL/Linux - reproducibly, from a script, adapting mainframe code to Linux program by program.
  • The Go interest engine passes the golden-master parity harness on every edge dataset.
  • That parity harness runs in CI as the cutover gate.

Wired but deficient not yet validated

  • The batch is wired to Postgres behind a storage seam, and the scheduled job injects the database credentials from the cluster's managed-Postgres secret. It builds, vets, and unit-tests green - but I call it deficient, because it has never run against a live cluster, and it reads empty tables until Postgres is seeded. Seeding the data and a live-cluster end-to-end run are the next step. Until then, I don't count it as working.

Scaffolded or decorative roadmap

  • The object store is a placeholder - ephemeral, demo credentials, and nothing writes to it yet. It marks the target for future off-site backup, no more.
  • Metrics: the monitoring stack scrapes cluster and node metrics, but the app doesn't emit its own yet - infrastructure observability, not application observability.
  • The cloud landing zone (Azure/AKS is the named target) is documented interface stubs, not real Terraform, and there's no continuous-deploy pipeline to the cloud. Version one is local-only, by design and by budget.
  • No real mainframe, an AI-directed "dev team," and synthetic data stand in for the real thing - and are labeled that way everywhere.

I could hide those seams. The honest version is more useful to anyone deciding whether to trust the rest of it - and it's the version that holds up in an interview.

How I actually work

Here's the part the code doesn't show. I'm not the one typing the implementation. AI agents are the development team; I direct them the way I'd direct a room of engineers - I hold the vision, make the business case, own the architecture calls, review the work, and keep it honest against specs and parity gates. The hands on the keyboard changed. The job of leading the build didn't.

Where I am hands-on is the part a DevOps job is actually about: the infrastructure and the enablement. I own the platform the team builds on - the Terraform, the cluster, the CI, the seams and the guardrails - and I make sure that enablement genuinely works, so the team can function instead of fighting the environment. That's not a footnote under the "real" work. On this kind of migration it is the work. The developers move fast because the ground under them is solid, and keeping it solid is my job.

The judgment calls were mine, and each one traces to a commit: host-passthrough CPU for the lab guests, pinning the libvirt provider below a breaking release so a mid-bring-up rewrite didn't derail the work, truncation over rounding to stay faithful to the COBOL, deferring a heavier workflow engine because a closed-world batch doesn't need it yet and adding it now would be complexity for the resume, not the system. Knowing which decisions are mine and which belong to someone else is most of the job - picking the production datastore in a real migration, for instance, is a business call for the people who own that risk, not mine to make. My job is to surface it with clean options, not to quietly decide it.

The reason I can direct work across COBOL, Go, Terraform, and Kubernetes without being the deepest expert in each is the same reason a good lead can: know enough of every art to direct it well. Musashi put it better - "know the ways of all professions" - and the precept right before it is "do not think dishonestly." Felt like the right project to build in the open.

Two things set my work apart, and I'll say them plainly. Today it's leverage. Against my peers, it's breadth - I'm wide, not deep on average, and I dig deep only where a problem actually needs it. Put those together and the breadth is enough to own a small-to-medium company's project edge to edge - business case, architecture, platform, delivery - and to carry it all the way to done, not just advise from the side. When executing it as one person is the right call for the business, the leverage is what makes that possible. The proof is two projects, not a claim: this modernization, stood up and run end to end, and Relytical - a real, full property-management SaaS (accounting, leasing, work orders, messaging, an automations engine) that I architected and shipped solo the same way. Two different domains, both owned edge to edge by one person.

And the leverage runs both ways - that's the actual mechanism, not a slogan. I lean on AI's strengths to cover my weaker areas, and I lean on my own strengths to cover AI's weaknesses. Human and AI as complements in both directions: the team moves faster and wider than I could alone, and I keep it honest, grounded, and pointed at the real problem in ways it can't do for itself. That two-way fit is what lets one person own and deliver work that normally takes a room - without pretending to be the deepest specialist in any single seat.

So I made the usually-invisible part visible on purpose. Why interest first, why truncate, what's deficient and why - it's all written down in the repo as decision records and a status page. Not a demo that hides the seams. A build you can audit.
